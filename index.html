Graph Theory

Every webpage that mentions you is a node. When we extract data from that page and feed it back into the system, it leads us to more pages — those connections are the edges.

One LinkedIn profile leads to a company page, which leads to a press mention, which leads to a university directory. The graph expands with every piece of data we collect. The more pages we find, the more pages we can reach.

Built on top of stable family records
and enrichment policies.

Why It Works
Simple rules,
compounding results.
Completely Repeatable
Run every policy a thousand times on the same person. You get the same result every time. No duplicates, no conflicts, no cleanup.
Data Only Grows
Nothing in the system ever gets overwritten or deleted. Every pass adds to what's already there. The profile only gets more complete.
The More It Knows, The More It Finds
Both policies get better with more context. Family discovery finds more people when enrichment data is present. Each cycle makes the whole system smarter.
Public Data Only
Every data point comes from publicly available sources. Social media, professional profiles, open directories. Nothing private, nothing scraped behind a login.
Family-Level Linking
When one family member's information changes — a new address, a new company — that update flows automatically to everyone in the household who shares it.
Works on Any Population
The architecture isn't specific to MIT. Same system, same policies — point it at any group of people and it builds the same structured, linked profiles.

About Us

Building intelligence
infrastructure at MIT.

Harpocrates is a private AI agent that constructs structured family and personal profiles using only publicly available data. It was built to demonstrate what's possible — and what's already happening.

The Project

The system follows a three-step process: start with a list of names, discover family connections, then enrich every person's profile with structured data from public sources. Two LLM-driven policies handle the intelligence work — one for finding family members, one for building detailed personal records. Both are designed to be run repeatedly without breaking anything, and both get smarter the more data they see.

The Architecture

At its core, Harpocrates is a modular entity resolution system. Family records serve as the central data structure — shared across family members, linked at the household level, and grown iteratively by each policy pass. The long-term goal is a full graph theory representation of digital footprints, where every webpage is a node and every discovered connection is an edge.

Why It Exists

Companies are already building systems like this — often with less transparency and less concern for the people in their databases. Harpocrates exists to make the mechanics of digital profiling legible. If you understand how the system works, you can understand how exposed you are.

Ethics & Purpose

Ethics*
and Purpose

This tool exists to make a point — and to give people the information they need to protect themselves.

The Problem

Right now, there are companies building highly organized databases of personal information using nothing but publicly available data. They scrape social media, professional networks, public records, and news mentions to construct detailed profiles of individuals and their families. Most of them don't have your best interests in mind.

The uncomfortable truth is that this isn't theoretical. It's happening, it's scalable, and most people have no idea how much of their life is already structured in someone else's database.

The Danger of Public Linking

When a parent posts a photo tagging their child's school, mentions their kid's name in a public comment, or shares a family event linking themselves to their children — they're not just sharing a moment. They're creating a permanent, traceable connection between two digital identities.

For an LLM agent, these connections are gold. A single post linking a parent to a child can open up an entirely new branch of discoverable information. The child's school leads to a sports roster. The roster leads to a coach. The coach leads to a list of other families. Every public breadcrumb makes the map more complete.

This is the real cost of oversharing — not embarrassment, but exposure at a structural level.

Hypernodes

Not all data points are equal. In the graph representation of someone's digital footprint, certain nodes carry disproportionate weight. We call these hypernodes — single pieces of public information that, if they didn't exist, would make large portions of the graph completely unreachable.

Think of a LinkedIn profile that connects your full name to your employer, your university, and your location. Remove that one page, and suddenly an LLM agent can't bridge from your professional life to your personal one. The graph falls apart.

The purpose of Harpocrates is to identify these hypernodes — to show individuals exactly which pieces of their public presence carry the most structural risk, and to let them make informed decisions about what to keep visible and what to take down.

Removing one hypernode disconnects an entire region of the graph

The Goal

Harpocrates isn't built to exploit — it's built to expose the mechanics. If people understand how these systems work, they can take control of their own digital footprint instead of leaving it to companies that won't tell them what they've found.

Empowerment through transparency. That's the purpose.
